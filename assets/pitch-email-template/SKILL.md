# Keyona Pitch Email Skill

Use this skill to write pitch emails where Keyona is the sender positioning someone else (the principal) for an
opportunity with a third-party gatekeeper (the recipient). Built from the Martha Underwood / Prismm podcast tour.
Portable across ventures and outreach types.

---

## Before writing anything: mandatory live verification

Before drafting, verify the following via live web search. Never rely on training knowledge or prior session research.

- **Current host/contact:** Confirm who is actively running the show, org, or platform right now. Search "[name/show] 2026."
- **Platform status:** Confirm it is still active and publishing within the last 60 days.
- **Recent episode or content:** Find one specific piece from the last 90 days to reference in the opener.
- **Booking contact:** Re-verify the submission email or form is live. Do not use a contact from a prior session without checking.

Document each check before writing the draft. If any check returns outdated info, update before proceeding.

---

## The frame

Every paragraph does one job. If it does not do its job, cut it. The sender is not the center of gravity. The recipient is.
The principal is the bridge between what the recipient already does and what they could do next.

The structure assumes the recipient is busy, sophisticated, and over-pitched. It earns attention by showing genuine
fluency in what the recipient is building, then offers a specific, useful contribution.

---

## The 7-part structure

### 1. Greeting
`Hi [First name],` or `Hi [First name 1] and [First name 2],`

Signal this is a human email. Name every active host. Skipping one reads as an oversight.

---

### 2. Commendation opener
**Length:** 2-3 sentences.
**Job:** Earn the right to keep writing. Demonstrate fluency in what the recipient specifically does well.

Formula:
> [Specific thing they do] is the reason I'm reaching out. [Platform] has [specific impact or reputation], and [that quality] is exactly why [this person / story / idea] belongs [on your show / in your portfolio / at your event].

Rules:
- Name a real, specific episode, article, investment, or pattern. Not "your amazing content" or "your incredible work."
- Do not lead with "I've been a long-time listener" or "I've been following you for years." Center the recipient's work, not your relationship to it.
- The commendation must flow directly into why you are reaching out. Do not flatter and then pivot.
- Use abundance framing ("you have earned this trust") not scarcity framing ("most shows don't cover this").

---

### 3. Principal intro
**Length:** 2 sentences max.
**Job:** Who is this person and what are they actually building, in plain English.

Formula:
> I support [Name], [Title] of [Company]. [They are building / She leads / He runs] [product or work] for [who it serves], [the clearest one-line outcome].

Rules:
- "I support" is preferred over "I'm writing on behalf of." It reads collaborative, not transactional.
- Describe the product by what it does for users, not by category tags.
- No adjectives the product has not earned. Save credentials for the next block.

---

### 4. Credentials bullets
**Format:** 3 bullets, parallel structure, one line each.
**Job:** Pre-empt the "is this person credible?" question.

Rules:
- Pick three credentials that are as different as possible from each other. One business, one validation, one proof-point.
- Prefer concrete nouns: "$1M federal EDGE 2.0 funding" not "well-funded." "$25B bank contract" not "major bank."
- If you do not have three strong credentials, cut the block entirely. Weak bullets damage credibility.

---

### 5. Origin story
**Length:** 2 sentences exactly.
**Job:** Convert the principal from a resume into a person with a stake.

Formula:
> [They] built [product] after [specific originating experience]. The result was [what the world looked like before the fix], which is exactly the problem [they are] now solving.

Rules:
- The origin must be specific and personal, not a market gap description.
- Tie the origin back to the present work at the end of paragraph 5 so it does not read as a memoir sidebar.
- No emotional coaching words: "unfortunately," "sadly," "tragically." The facts carry the weight.

---

### 6. Why for your audience
**Length:** 3-5 sentences.
**Job:** Explicitly connect the principal's work to what the recipient's audience actually cares about. This is the paragraph that closes deals. It must be fully rewritten for every recipient.

Formula:
> What makes [them] a strong fit for your audience is [the specific angle]. [One elaboration sentence]. While [product/work] sits inside [one context], the impact is personal: [what it changes for the audience member].

Rules:
- Customize this paragraph entirely per recipient. Do not reuse phrasing from another pitch.
- If the principal has a dual lens (understands both sides of a market, has crossed a category line), name it here.
- Do not use: "synergy," "at the intersection of," "uniquely positioned."
- The principal's name should appear at most twice in the full email. Use pronouns after that.
- Include a relevant statistic here if one strengthens the connection to the audience. Verify the stat via live search before using it.

---

### 7. Alignment close and ask
**Length:** One alignment sentence, one question, sign-off.
**Job:** Hand the recipient a clean mental model for saying yes, then give them one easy thing to say yes to.

Formula:
> That balance aligns with what your audience consistently shows up for: [one phrase].
>
> Would [principal's first name]'s story be a good fit for [an episode / a feature / a call]?
>
> Best,
> [Sender first name]

Rules:
- The ask is always a yes/no question. No scheduler links, no multiple options, no "let me know if you'd like to learn more."
- If the recipient's submission process is unknown, close with: "If this is a good fit, I'd love to know how you prefer to receive guest submissions. I'm also available for a quick conversation at [scheduling link]."
- Sign-off is "Best," only. Not "Cheers," "Warmly," or anything performative.

---

## Structural guardrails (invariant across all versions)

- No em-dashes. Use commas, colons, semicolons, or period-new-sentence.
- No links in the body. If a link is needed, hyperlink the principal's name in-client before sending.
- No emojis.
- No "I'd love to connect," "synergy," "circle back," or "quick call."
- No scheduler link unless the close formula above requires it.
- Subject line: `Guest pitch: [Principal name] of [Company]` or `Guest pitch: [Principal name] for [Platform name]`. For non-podcast outreach, replace "Guest pitch" with the role word: "Intro," "Collab," "Invitation."
- Word count target: 230-260 words in the body. Under 230 reads thin; over 260 reads self-indulgent.
- Plain text first. Bullets render as `- item` in Gmail. No markdown in the actual send.
- One principal per email. Two principals = two emails.
- Principal's name appears at most twice in the body. Pronouns after that.

## Portability across outreach contexts

The 7-part structure is constant. Swap the job content per context:

| Context | #2 commendation targets | #3 principal framing | #6 translation angle |
|---|---|---|---|
| Podcast guest pitch | A recent episode, a host POV | "[Name] is building / leads..." | Why their story pressure-tests what the audience already engages with |
| Investor intro | Recent portfolio move, thesis post | "[Name] is raising for..." | Why this deal fits their thesis, not yours |
| Event speaker pitch | Last year's program, a panel theme | "[Name] is [current role/work]" | Why their talk lands with the delegate profile |
| Partnership outreach | A product launch, a public initiative | "[Name] leads [function] at [Co.]" | Where the two orgs' work compounds |
| Media / reporter pitch | A recent piece, a beat they cover | "[Name] is building / leading..." | Why the story sharpens a trend they already track |
| Advisor / board recruit | A company's recent move, a problem it solves | "I support [Name], [role], who is exploring..." | Why the recruit's expertise matches a visible gap |

What stays constant: the 7-part skeleton, commendation-before-ask rhythm, three different concrete credentials, two-sentence origin with present-tense tie-back, translation move in paragraph 6, single-question close, all structural guardrails.

What changes: the specific evidence in paragraph 2, the product sentence in paragraph 3, the three credentials in paragraph 4, the originating experience in paragraph 5, the audience-specific angle in paragraph 6, the ask verb in the close.

---

## Tone rules

- Warm but not gushing. Direct but not terse.
- Read every sentence as if the recipient is your most skeptical peer in the space. If a sentence would make them roll their eyes, cut or rewrite it.
- Never start a sentence with "Just." Never use "simply" as a softener.
- Contractions are fine in greeting and close. Avoid them in credentials and commendation paragraphs, where they flatten gravitas.
- Do not name yourself until the sign-off. The email is about the principal and the recipient.

---

## Failure modes

- Commendation reads generic: you have not named a specific thing. Go find one.
- Product sentence has three adjectives: you are selling, not describing. Cut to the function.
- Credentials do not stack a story: pick one business credential, one validation credential, one proof-point credential.
- Origin is a paragraph: trim to the incident and the link-back.
- Paragraph 6 feels copy-pasted: rewrite it entirely for this recipient.
- Close offers too many options: one yes/no question, always.

---

## Pre-send checklist

- [ ] Live verification completed for all host, status, and contact info
- [ ] Greeting names every active host
- [ ] Commendation cites something specific and real, verified live
- [ ] Principal's work described in plain English, no jargon
- [ ] Three credentials, concrete, different
- [ ] Origin story is two sentences and ties to present work
- [ ] Paragraph 6 is fully customized for this recipient
- [ ] Close is one yes/no question or the humble submission variant
- [ ] No em-dashes, emojis, links, scheduler link, or "synergy"
- [ ] Body is 230-260 words
- [ ] Subject line is functional, not clever
